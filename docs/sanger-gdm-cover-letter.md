## My Grand Challenge: Generative Virtual Cells for Underrepresented Populations

The Grand Challenge I would tackle: **build a generative virtual-cell foundation model that closes the gap between current atlases (heavily skewed toward European-ancestry, well-resourced reference data) and the populations and pathogens whose biology we most need to understand for global health impact.**

The technical novelty is in four parts. First, integrating Sanger's atlas-scale cellular genomics with the multi-modal architectures I have shipped in industry — knowledge graphs as structural priors, free text from literature as semantic anchors, omics + imaging as parallel modalities under self-supervised objectives. Second, evaluating not just held-out reconstruction but counterfactual perturbation — does the model give scientifically reasonable answers when asked "what if this gene were knocked down in this cell type from this individual?". Third, and most importantly for the GDM mandate of underexplored AI applications, explicitly evaluating and correcting for population-shift and species-shift in the latent space, so that an in-silico-derived hypothesis about, say, a P. falciparum variant in a Malawian patient is anchored in data the model has actually seen rather than extrapolated unsafely from a North American reference.

Fourth — and the area I would most like to push the field on — building an **agentic discovery layer** on top of the foundation model, walled in by inference-time engineering so that hallucination is contained to scientifically tolerable failure modes. The naive failure of agentic LLMs in biology is well documented: an agent invents a gene name, fabricates a citation, or proposes a perturbation that doesn't exist in any cell line. The engineering response is to refuse to let the agent free-generate over the things that matter. Constrained decoding restricted to a closed vocabulary of valid Ensembl IDs, HGNC symbols and ChEMBL identifiers; tool-call verification against Sanger's own structured resources (Cellular Genomics atlases, Tree of Life genomes, Parasites & Microbes pathogen catalogues) so that every claim is round-tripped through real data before the agent emits it; retrieval grounding to a curated literature corpus with citation-level attribution; verifier-model ensembles that score each hypothesis for biological plausibility before it leaves the agent. The result is an agent that proposes follow-up experiments — knockdowns to run, populations to oversample, pathogens to sequence — but only inside a sandbox where every leaf claim is checkable. This is, in my view, the missing piece between "an LLM that talks about biology" and "an autonomous research collaborator". It is also the dimension of the project where the GDM mentorship is most directly useful: the inference-engineering and constrained-generation work at DeepMind on AlphaFold's confidence calibration and on the AlphaProof verifier loop is exactly the lineage I want to bring into genomics.

Three specific deliverables I would target in year one: (i) a Sanger-anchored multi-modal foundation model trained jointly across Cellular Genomics atlases and the Tree of Life programme's non-model-organism data, with public benchmarks for population-coverage equity; (ii) a perturbation-prediction benchmark in collaboration with Sanger's Generative Genomics programme that scores models on diverse-ancestry validation cohorts rather than convenience samples; (iii) an open-source **agentic discovery harness** with built-in hallucination-suppression — constrained decoding, tool-call verification, retrieval grounding, verifier ensembles — that other Sanger researchers can wrap around their own foundation models. Year two onward expands this to pathogen genomes (Parasites & Microbes), where both the population-shift problem and the agentic-discovery upside are sharpest.

## Outreach: a year-one capacity-building programme for Genomics+AI in LMICs

The outreach fund is the part of this Fellowship I am most excited about, and I have a concrete plan rather than a vague aspiration. My intent is a recurring, in-country **"Foundation Models for Genomics" workshop series**, run twice a year at LMIC partner institutions, each lasting one week and producing graduates who can fine-tune and evaluate ML models on local genomic data. The model is the one I have personally taught — annual deep-learning courses at EMBL-EBI to 40+ researchers — adapted for compute-constrained settings: containerised pipelines, transfer learning from publicly hosted weights, datasets sourced where possible from the host country's own collections.

The first workshop I would scope is in collaboration with the **H3ABioNet/H3Africa** community, who have spent over a decade building African genomic infrastructure but have systematically been underserved by the foundation-model wave. Year-two and year-three editions would expand to South-East Asia (probably anchored at the MORU collaboration in Bangkok or NUS in Singapore) and Latin America. Each edition leaves behind: (i) a containerised teaching environment that runs on modest hardware, (ii) a small number of fine-tuned community models on locally-relevant tasks, and (iii) a peer cohort of trainers who can run subsequent editions independently. I would budget the fund deliberately for travel cost-sharing for participants who cannot self-fund, because that is where most "open" programmes silently fail.

Beyond the workshops, I would use the ambassadorial part of the role to push for honest evaluation practices in published genomics-AI work — particularly insisting that benchmarks include population-diversity stratifications, in line with the Grand Challenge framing above. I have done versions of this kind of community-shaping work informally already, founding the Virtual Cell Journal Club at Valence and contributing to napari, the Bioimage Model Zoo, and the AI4LIFE €5M federated bioimage AI infrastructure grant.
